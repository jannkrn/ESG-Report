# ESG Report: Microsoft

## 1. Company Overview

Microsoft is a global technology company that develops and provides software, cloud infrastructure, digital platforms, devices, gaming products and online services. Its mission is to empower every person and every organization on the planet to achieve more. The company positions itself as a provider of secure, trusted and innovative technology solutions for individuals, businesses and public-sector organizations.

Microsoft generates revenue through cloud-based solutions, software licensing, support services, online advertising, consulting and device sales. Its business is organized into three main segments: Productivity and Business Processes, Intelligent Cloud and More Personal Computing. Productivity and Business Processes includes Microsoft 365, LinkedIn, Dynamics 365 and related cloud services. Intelligent Cloud includes Azure, GitHub, Nuance Healthcare cloud services and server products such as SQL Server and Windows Server. More Personal Computing includes Windows, Surface devices, Xbox, gaming content and search advertising.

A central strategic focus is the expansion of cloud computing and artificial intelligence. Azure is Microsoft’s core cloud platform, providing infrastructure-as-a-service and platform-as-a-service solutions for enterprises, developers and public-sector customers. Microsoft’s strong position in cloud and AI makes data centres, energy consumption, water use, responsible AI, cybersecurity and data protection especially relevant ESG topics.

## 2. Environmental

Microsoft’s environmental ESG profile is strongly shaped by its cloud and AI infrastructure. The company aims to replace its electricity use with 100% renewable energy and has increased its renewable energy procurement significantly. This is particularly relevant because data centres and AI workloads require large amounts of electricity.

A major environmental focus is the efficiency of data centres. Microsoft has studied the environmental impact of different data-centre cooling technologies, including air cooling, cold plates and immersion cooling. According to the available information, switching from traditional air cooling to cold plates can reduce greenhouse-gas emissions and energy demand by approximately 15% and water consumption by 30–50% over the data-centre lifecycle. Cold plates and immersion cooling technologies can reduce greenhouse-gas emissions by 15–21%, energy demand by 15–20% and water consumption by 31–52% compared with air cooling.

Another important finding is that switching data centres to 100% renewable energy sources could reduce greenhouse-gas emissions by 85–90%, regardless of the cooling technology used. This shows that renewable energy procurement is a key lever for reducing Microsoft’s environmental impact.

However, several limitations remain. Across the digital sector, Scope 3 emissions represent a major share of total emissions. These indirect emissions are difficult to measure because they depend on suppliers, customers and the wider value chain. The available evidence also highlights transparency gaps in the digital sector: only about half of the assessed digital companies provide complete greenhouse-gas inventories covering Scope 1, 2 and 3 emissions, and only a smaller group submits these inventories for third-party verification. For Microsoft, the growing environmental impact of AI and cloud infrastructure makes detailed reporting on data-centre energy use, water use and Scope 3 emissions particularly important.

## 3. Social

Microsoft’s social ESG activities focus on diversity and inclusion, accessibility, human rights, employee development, community empowerment and digital safety. The company states that every employee sets an annual Diversity and Inclusion Core Priority goal, which is intended to create accountability and support an inclusive workplace culture.

Employee survey results indicate a positive internal perception of inclusion. According to the available information, 83.5% of employees observed intentional allyship actions, and 81.2% agreed that Microsoft is diverse and inclusive. Representation data shows 31.6% global women, 8.0% US Hispanic/Latinx employees, 6.6% US Black/African American employees and 27.2% global women in technical roles.

Supplier diversity is another relevant social topic. In fiscal year 2024, Microsoft spent more than $18 billion with over 700 diverse and underrepresented businesses globally. This indicates that the company links social responsibility not only to its workforce, but also to its supply chain.

Microsoft also invests in education, skills and accessibility. The TEALS program has supported computer science education for more than 100,000 students since 2009, with a focus on inclusion by race, gender and geography. Accessibility initiatives include AI skilling programs with more than 5 million participants and technologies such as the Adaptive Joystick for Xbox, sign language detection in Teams, low-vision keyboards for Surface and AI-powered visual descriptions in Windows.

Human rights and digital safety are also important social topics. Microsoft describes human rights due diligence processes aligned with international standards, including risk assessments, stakeholder engagement and independent evaluations. The company also works on online safety, including child protection, privacy and measures against harmful or deceptive AI-generated content.

## 4. Governance

Microsoft’s governance framework is designed to support trust, accountability and oversight across financial and non-financial risks. The Board of Directors oversees topics such as responsible AI, privacy, digital safety, cybersecurity, environmental and social matters.

Board independence is a central governance feature. According to the available information, 11 of 12 board nominees are independent. Key committees include the Audit Committee, Compensation Committee, Environmental, Social and Public Policy Committee, and Governance and Nominating Committee. These committees are composed of independent directors and oversee areas such as financial reporting, executive compensation, ESG matters, risk management and board effectiveness.

The Environmental, Social and Public Policy Committee oversees non-financial regulatory risks and management policies related to environmental and social issues, including climate change, privacy, responsible AI, human rights and responsible sourcing. This is particularly relevant because Microsoft operates in areas where AI ethics, data protection and cybersecurity are material governance risks.

Executive compensation is designed to align management incentives with long-term strategic goals and shareholder interests. Microsoft also maintains a clawback policy for executive officers and senior leaders, which strengthens accountability. Shareholder rights include one vote per share, annual director elections, majority voting in uncontested elections and proxy access.

Responsible AI is a key governance priority. Microsoft states that it aims to design and deploy AI responsibly, with safety and security considered from the beginning. Governance structures are intended to support responsible AI principles, regulatory compliance and risk management across the company.

## 5. Key ESG Risks and Data Gaps

The main environmental risks relate to the rapid growth of cloud and AI infrastructure. Data centres require significant electricity and water, and the environmental impact of AI workloads is difficult to measure precisely. More granular disclosure on data-centre performance, AI-specific workloads, Power Usage Effectiveness, Water Usage Effectiveness and Scope 3 emissions would improve transparency.

In the social area, key risks relate to human rights, digital safety and the use of Microsoft technologies by customers. Even if Microsoft has human rights due diligence processes, risks can arise when cloud, software or AI products are used in ways that the company cannot fully control.

Governance risks are linked to responsible AI, cybersecurity, data protection and regulatory compliance. As Microsoft expands its AI and cloud business, the company faces increasing expectations from regulators, customers, investors and society to manage these risks transparently and effectively.

## 6. Overall Assessment

Microsoft shows a broad and structured ESG profile. Environmental efforts focus on renewable energy, data-centre efficiency and lower-impact cooling technologies. Social initiatives cover diversity and inclusion, accessibility, skills development, supplier diversity and human rights. Governance structures include independent board oversight, shareholder rights, executive accountability and responsible AI governance.

At the same time, important limitations remain. The strongest ESG challenges are connected to the environmental footprint of cloud and AI infrastructure, Scope 3 emissions, water use, human rights risks in technology deployment and the need for more granular transparency. Overall, Microsoft reports significant ESG initiatives, but the company’s future ESG performance will depend on whether its climate, AI governance and transparency commitments can keep pace with the rapid growth of cloud and artificial intelligence.

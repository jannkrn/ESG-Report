# Literature Review and Hypotheses

## ESG reporting

ESG reporting describes corporate disclosures on environmental, social and governance-related impacts, risks, targets and management approaches.

Environmental reporting can include greenhouse gas emissions, energy use, renewable energy, water consumption, waste management, climate-related risks and climate targets.

Social reporting can include workforce size, diversity and inclusion, employee health and safety, labor relations, human rights, supply-chain standards, product responsibility and community engagement.

Governance reporting can include board composition and independence, executive compensation, audit and risk oversight, anti-corruption policies, data protection, shareholder rights and regulatory compliance.

## Relevant reporting frameworks

The Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) provides widely used standards for reporting an organisation’s impacts on the economy, environment and people.

The European Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) requires companies in scope to report according to the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS).

For climate-related financial information, the recommendations of the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures (TCFD) remain influential. Their main principles are incorporated into the IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards, especially IFRS S2.

## Quality criteria for an ESG report

A meaningful ESG report should:

* cover all relevant Environmental, Social and Governance topics;
* include company-specific risks, targets and measures;
* provide quantitative information where available;
* distinguish between achievements, future targets and unresolved risks;
* disclose relevant controversies or regulatory issues;
* explain the scope, time horizon and limitations of reported metrics;
* use reliable and traceable sources.

## Limitations of ESG disclosures

ESG information can be difficult to compare across companies because reporting standards, metrics, materiality assessments and calculation methods differ. ESG ratings may also diverge because rating providers differ in their scope, measurement methods and weighting of ESG indicators.

Therefore, a company’s official ESG report should not be treated as a fully objective representation of sustainability performance. It is still useful as the benchmark for analysing differences between official disclosure and an AI-generated report.

## Hypotheses

**H1:** The AI-generated ESG reports will cover broad ESG topics, but will contain fewer company-specific quantitative metrics than the official ESG reports.

**H2:** The AI-generated reports will be more dependent on the availability and quality of publicly collected input data. Topics for which few independent sources are available are more likely to be missing or less detailed.

**H3:** A single reusable workflow will not perform equally well for all four companies. Amazon and Zalando are strongly exposed to supply-chain and retail-related ESG topics, while Microsoft and SAP have greater relevance of data centres, digital products, data protection and corporate governance.
